Explore what Korean typography could be in the digital age, where screen, online communication, animation and interaction are considered common-sense.

Typography & Coding

We decided, to be able to explore the digital media, it was necessary for students to learn to code, instead of relying on any existing tools.

We do use computer softwares to design, but when we directly work with the code, it provides interesting possibilities.

Working with systems and rules that create letterforms.

Working with code means working with rules and systems that create letterforms, instead of working directly with the forms. We have to tell the machine how to do things.

Of course, type design requires a manual adjustment of each letterform, so computer programming is not the answer for every situation.

Working with variables and their range.

Ekstasy Type Club by Dae In Chung

And we also work with the variables, and they have a range - minimum and maximum values. And that range is on the continuum. We don't have to break them into a few weights or shapes. We set variables for the things we want to control, and set the min/max values. If we push too hard, the forms will no longer be readable.

In this example, there are about 10 different variables with sliders. And the combination it can make is almost endless. Of course, not all the combinations will look good.

Mapping data to letterform

  • distance
  • temperature
  • time of the day
  • nuance
  • emotion
  • and more...

These variables can create a relationship between inputs and outputs. We can map the data to letterform.

There are some data that are already presented as numeric form, such as distance, temperature or time of the day, etc.

There are other types of data that are more subjective such as nuances and emotions. And they will have to be convertred to numbers that machine can understand.

The variables can change. Instead of having fixed values or forms, they can respond to different conditions and conveying different nuances.

Hangul

Korean written language

So, those were some of the inspirations, and before I talk about the courses themselves, I want to talk about Hangul, Korean written language system as it has a unique characteristic, and because this is what we used in our courses.

Hangul was invented by King Sejong in 1443.

Hangul is interesting because there is a single person who designed it. We had a spoken language, but no written language so Koreans had to borrow Chinese characters. Korean and Chinese are very different, so it was not the ideal situation, and only the priviledged knew how to write. King Sejong wanted to make Hangul available for everyone.

Five basic consonants are designed after speech organs

There are five basic consonants that are designed after speech organs.

'ieung' is silent at the initial position. It's mostly used with vowels that follow.

g, k

'giyeok' resembles the shape of the throat and the tongue when making /g/ sound.

n

'nieun' resembles the shape of the tongue touching the roof of the mouth when making /n/ sound.

m

'mieum' resembles the shape of the mouth (front) when making /m/ sound.

s

'siot' resembles the shape of the tooth when making /s/ sound.

-ng

'ieung' resembles the shape of the throat. It is silent most of the time, and used with vowels.

Other consonants

Other consonants are created by adding strokes to those five basic shapes based on how strong the sound is.

Add strokes based on the strength of the sound

Here is 'mieum' with /m/ sound. Do you think you can imagine how we can extend the form by adding strokes to create /b/, /p/, /pp/ sounds?

Add strokes based on the strength of the sound

This is how it's done in Hangul. It's interesting to look at /b/ and /pp/. The same shape is repeated twice to express the strength.

Syllable

Modular Design

I will skip over the vowels and talk about how a syllable is made in Hangul.

A syllable takes a modular design. There could be two or three components. Initial consonant + medial vowel + final consonant.

Here, it is creating /na/ sound.

When we add 'mieum' as the final consonant, it creates /nam/ sound.

We now replace the first consonant to 'giyeok', and it creates /gam/ sound.

10,000+

In theory, there are more than 10,000+ letters we can make using this modular system. But in reality, we do not use many of them, they exist as a possibility.

This modular system allows for interesting experiments.

Time for an exercise!

(about 15m)

Think of a turtle at the center of paper.

You will create instructions for it to follow and move.

When the turtle moves, it leaves a trace.

I will give you a shape to draw.

Discuss as a group to decide what instructions/commands to use. Keep the instructions simple - a word and a number for each.

Describe how, not what to draw.

"Draw a big rectangle."

"Draw a lowercase b"

Describe how, not what to draw. Don't say "draw a big rectangle" because it relies on what we already know. Same with "draw a lowercase b."

Half the group will give instructions.
The other half will execute them.

First, form a group, and discuss to come up with some instructions. Then, I will give you a shape to draw. 

  TO SQUARE
    FORWARD 100
    RIGHT 90
    FORWARD 100
    RIGHT 90
    FORWARD 100
    RIGHT 90
    FORWARD 100
    RIGHT 90
  END

from Mindstorm by Seymour Papert

This exercise is inspired by the book, Mindstorm and LOGO programming environment by Seymour Papert.

Here, we see two simple commands for the turtle to perform. And we can give this series of instruction a name; SQUARE. So, anytime we call SQUARE "function", it will perform the same instructions. 

  TO SQUARE
    REPEAT 4
    FORWARD 100
    RIGHT 90
  END

from Mindstorm by Seymour Papert

And because the same forward/right commands are repeated, we can tell it to repeat it 4 times. 

  TO SQUARE:SIZE
    REPEAT 4
    FORWARD :SIZE
    RIGHT 90
  END

from Mindstorm by Seymour Papert

Now, instead of telling the turtle to draw the same square every time, we can abstract the distnce it moves and make it into a variable, SIZE. Then, we can call the SQUARE function with different numbers to draw squares in different sizes.

  • Parameterize
  • Function
  • Modularize
  • Iteration

With this simple exercise, we can learn many important design concepts that are already familiar to software engineers.

1. Instead of saying 'draw a BIG square,' we parameterize the SIZE to respond to different conditons.

2. The way we command the turtle, different actions it can take is a function. We also abstract the function SQUARE.

3. Each instruction is simple; move or turn. We can break down the complex instruction into smaller pieces.

4. We can develop the logic and test and repeat to improve it. That is an iteration.

Course 1: Type+Media, 2015

Instuctors: Dae In Chung & Jiwon Lee

Now, I want to talk about the 2 courses. The T+M was taught by myself and Jiwon in 2015 in Kookmin University, Seoul, South Korea.

Objectives

  • Fundamentals of programming
  • Systematic way of thinking and working
  • Type that responds to conditions

Our objectives were first, 1. teaching the fundamentals of programming because almost everybody in class didn't have any prior experience with coding. So we had to start from scratch.

2. Second, we wanted the students to get used to the systematic way of thinking and working, which is required to work with programming and creating a system.

And lastly, we wanted them to learn by doing. Instead of just doing simple exercises, we asked students to create individual interactive projects by the end of the semester.

Tools

  • Processing has a large user base and learning resources
  • Course website to help self-learning out of class.

    • We chose Processing programming language because it already has a large user base of visual artists and designers. It also has a lot of learning resources both online and offline.

    We also created a course website with many examples. It was important that students have resources outside the course for self-learning.

    Course Topics Overview

    • Conditional Design
    • Drawing with Code
    • Variables & Functions
    • Individual Projects

    In terms of course topics, we started a semester with conditional design exercises, which I will talk about a little later.

    Then, we started to learn to draw with code. And with simple exercises, we learned how to work with variables and functions

    We ended the semester with individual projects, which we exhibited in a gallery setting.

    Conditional Design

    ...Our work focuses on processes rather than products: things that adapt to their environment, emphasize change and show difference...
    — conditionaldesign.org

    As many of you may already know, conditional design is an exercise to come up with simple instructions for other people to follow. It was started by 4 dutch designers.

    Here is an excerpt from their manifesto. "Our work focuses..."

    conditionaldesign.org

    At the bottom of the image, there is an instruction for the group to follow taking turns.

    conditionaldesign.org

    Conditional design describes the process, "how" to draw, not the final outcome. And because a word can be interpreted differently, the end result is not always the same.

    Drawing with Code

    Then, we set out to understand the coordinate system and draw shapes where we want.

    It is an interesting and difficult process because everything must be expressed with numbers. It gives us a chance to realize how everything visual is translated to numbers and vice versa.

    Must have intentions

    When students first start to draw with code, it is so easy and fun to rely on random values to create unexpected things. That is a great experiment on its own, but we asked students to work with the intentions in what they create because type should be used to communicate something.

    So, it is a matter of control vs. chaos. We create variables and control them, whether that's a value or a range or a change over time.

    Course 2: DesignTech Workshop

    Instructors: Jiwon Lee & Daegwon Kim

    After we taught T+M, we thought about improving it a little bit, and offered DesignTech Workshop just this Summer. It was taught by Jiwon and Daegwon, who is a professional software engineer. I will talk about the course on behalf of them.

    Objectives

    • Fundamentals of programming
    • Systematic way of thinking and working
    • Type that responds to conditions
    • Practical applications

    We mostly had the same objectives. There are not many designers who already has the foundations in programming. So every time we teach, we have to start from scratch.

    Same systematic thinking and working, and creating type that responds to conditions.

    We had one added goal, which is to make the projects usable in a real situation. We wanted the end result to be practical applications.

    Tools

    Moved from Processing to p5.js. It is similar to Processing. It works on the browser and mobile devices.

    We moved from using Processing to P5.js. It is very similar to Processing. As a javascript library, it works directly on the browser and mobile devices without any extra setup, which could mean we can reach a wider audience.

    Course Topics Overview

    • Conditional Design
    • Selective topics on programming
    • Individual projects

    Course topics were almost the same.

    This time when we did conditional design, instead of creating random visuals, we built instructions that will actually create Korean letterforms.

    Programming fundamentals

    • function — type design
    • coordinates — type setting
    • variable, loop, conditional — animation, interaction
    • automata — Hangul character input

    Instead of going over every detail about programming like a textbook, we tried to relate as much as possible to typography.

    It was about setting the right context such as functions are used to design type, coordinates to set type, variable/loop/conditional are used to create animation and interaction.

    Automata was used to enable Hangul input from users. Because of Hangul's unique combination system, it is technically more difficult to display text. We used an open-source automata algorithm for that.

    Things We Learned

    • Designer's perspective
    • Team teaching
    • Design process
    • Communication

    By teaching two courses, here are a few things we learned along the way.

    1. First, we learned that it was important to teach and approach from a designer's perspective. Making the difficult process of learning code a little more friendly and using the relevant examples for the designers.

    2. Team teaching was also quite effective. For T+M, Jiwon was more traditionally trained designer and I was more interested in animation and technology. So, we could balance our opinions and provide different perspectives to students. And for DesignTech workshop, Daegwon is a software engineer, so he was able to provide a better technical assistance.

    3. It also gave us a chance to think about our design process. Moving the focus from making individual outcomes to designing a system. There are also many design concepts that programmers use such as modularization and iteration that also help the design process.

    4. We also had a chance to think about communication between machine and designer as well as communication between this type of work and the audience.

    Challenges

    • Programming is hard...
    • One semester is not long enough.
    • Loss of interest; no further opportunity
    • Nobody to ask questions.

    We also had many challenges that we do not have all the answers yet.

    1. Programming is really hard. Just the thought of the numbers and mathematics was enough to make students overwhelmed and be afraid even before they try it themlselves.

    2. One semester was not long enough to cover both typography and coding, especially because we had to start from scratch in teaching coding. Students were left under a lot of pressure and it was hard to plan ahead when they were not sure if they could make it or not.

    3. After finishing the semester, I (unofficially) tracked the students from the class to see if they continued to work in the area, and found only a very few were still working with the code. When there is no connection to other courses in the curriculum, it is difficult to keep the interests. Students usually have very tight deadlines, and this way of working, which requires a lot of planning, abstraction and debugging, is not very attractive.

    4. I always say to students that Google is your best friend. But, when they are first starting, even knowing how to ask the right questions can be difficult. And when I look around, not a lot of graphic design program has a full-time faculty member who is knowledgible in this intersection, so students do not have a resource they can use in school.

    What machines can do and what designers can do

    Recent developments in technology and machine learning challenge designers in many ways. We will have to think about what machines can do and what designers can do.

    Netflix thumbnail generating algorithm

    Netflix has lots and lots of movie thumbnails to create for every movie they have in the collection. And they developed an algorithm where the machine can understand the main figure in an image, not just a human figure, but anything that it thinks is the main object. And it can crop the image in multiple formats without cutting the main figure. It can also place the text not covering the main figure.

    If the real designers were to do this, what does it mean to cut thousands of photos one by one and place the text in the right place? How long is it going to take?

    Erik Bernhardsson

    In this example, Erik Bernhardsson, a software engineer, collected 50,000 fonts online using a script. Then, he trained the machine with those 50k fonts.

    On the left image, he showed the machine all the characters of a font except one, and made it guess by looking at all the other characters. The actual character from a font is on the left, and the one the machine guessed is on the right. whether the machine correctly guessed or not may not be very important, but looking at these shapes may give us new ideas.

    On the right image, He gave four As on each corner, and have the machine interpolate the inbetweens.

    We could imagine that the machine can be useful in designing a typeface, or at least we can ask it to visualize some possibilities.
